# Zetto Branding Strategy

## Brand Essence

**One line:** The gym for your Japanese.

**Core tension the brand lives in:** Warm human connection meets disciplined training structure. Zetto is not cold or clinical. It's not soft or coddling. It's the energy of a coach who genuinely cares about you and expresses that care by refusing to let you slack off.

## Name

**Zetto** (ゼット) is the Japanese pronunciation of the letter Z. The name carries two meanings simultaneously:

1. **A to Z.** Complete journey. From first word to fluent conversation. Nothing left out.
2. **Phonetic identity.** The name itself is a Japanese word, pronounced with Japanese phonetics. Using the app starts with saying its name correctly. ゼット. Not "zeh-toe." ゼット.

The name is short, punchy, globally pronounceable, and sounds like something with an edge to it. It doesn't sound like a children's toy, a social network, or a meditation app. It sounds like a tool built for a specific job.

## Brand Positioning

### Category

Language training app. Not language learning app. The word "training" is deliberate. Learning is passive. Training is active, progressive, measurable. Zetto trains your Japanese the way a program trains your body.

### Positioning Statement

For adults who want to actually speak Japanese, Zetto is the voice-first training app that measures your real-time production ability and progressively increases difficulty based on performance, not participation.

### Competitive Landscape

| App | Positioning | Weakness Zetto exploits |
|-----|------------|------------------------|
| Duolingo | Gamified learning for everyone | Passive recognition, no production pressure, streak addiction replaces actual skill |
| WaniKani | Kanji memorization via SRS | Reading-only, no speaking, no conversation |
| Pimsleur | Audio-based spaced repetition | Scripted, no adaptation, no real-time measurement |
| iTalki | Live tutors | Expensive, scheduling friction, quality varies wildly |
| Anki | Flashcard SRS | Pure memorization, no context, no conversation, ugly |
| Busuu | Community + curriculum | Shallow production, social features distract from skill building |

**Zetto's unique position:** The only app that measures your spoken production latency in real time and uses it to drive progressive overload. Nobody else is doing acoustic telemetry for language acquisition.

### Audience Segments

**Primary: The Serious Self-Studier**
Adults 20-45 who've tried other apps and plateaued or quit. They know they need more than Duolingo but can't afford or commit to a tutor. They're willing to work hard if the system is honest with them. Many have a concrete reason: upcoming trip, partner is Japanese, relocation, career.

**Secondary: The Heritage Reconnector**
People with Japanese family connections who understand some Japanese passively but can't produce it. The receptive-productive gap is their defining characteristic. Zetto's production-first approach directly addresses their specific problem.

**Tertiary: The Ambitious Beginner**
Total beginners who chose Zetto over Duolingo because the positioning resonated. They don't want gamification. They want results. Often the type of person who also tracks their gym progress meticulously.

### What Zetto Is Not For

People who want to "dabble." People looking for a fun 5-minute distraction. People who want to feel good about learning without actually doing the work. Zetto doesn't punish these people; it just isn't designed for them, and the brand shouldn't try to attract them.

## Visual Identity

### Design Philosophy: Disciplined Warmth

The visual system borrows from two traditions and fuses them into something that feels neither fully Western nor fully Japanese:

1. **Japanese functional design.** The clarity of Tokyo transit signage. The precision of muji. Information-dense but never cluttered. Every element earns its space.
2. **Athletic training aesthetics.** The data-forward interfaces of Whoop, TrainingPeaks, Strava. Performance metrics displayed cleanly. Progress that's visible and honest.

The result should feel like a well-designed training dashboard that happens to be teaching you Japanese. Not a language app. Not a game. A tool with purpose.

### Color Palette

**Primary: Ink Black + Paper White**

The foundation is high-contrast black and white. This references Japanese calligraphy (sumi ink on washi paper) and signals seriousness without being gloomy. Most of the UI lives in this space.

| Role | Color | Hex | Usage |
|------|-------|-----|-------|
| Background (dark mode) | Sumi Black | `#0D0D0D` | Primary background |
| Background (light mode) | Washi White | `#F5F2ED` | Warm off-white, not clinical |
| Text (dark mode) | Warm White | `#E8E4DF` | Body text on dark |
| Text (light mode) | Ink Black | `#1A1A1A` | Body text on light |

**Accent: Vermillion**

A single accent color, pulled from Japanese torii gates and hanko stamps. Used sparingly for active states, progress indicators, and critical UI moments.

| Role | Color | Hex | Usage |
|------|-------|-----|-------|
| Primary accent | Vermillion | `#D94032` | Active states, progress, CTA buttons |
| Accent hover | Deep Vermillion | `#B8342A` | Hover/pressed states |
| Accent muted | Faded Vermillion | `#D9403220` | Backgrounds, subtle highlights |

**Functional Colors**

| Role | Color | Hex | Usage |
|------|-------|-----|-------|
| Success | Matcha | `#5B8C5A` | Correct responses, milestone completion |
| Warning | Amber | `#D4A03C` | Struggle indicators, latency warnings |
| Subtle | Stone | `#6B6560` | Secondary text, disabled states |
| Surface | Tatami | `#2A2725` | Cards, elevated surfaces (dark mode) |
| Surface | Rice Paper | `#EDE8E0` | Cards, elevated surfaces (light mode) |

**Color rules:**
- Never more than one accent color on screen at a time. Vermillion is the only pop of color against the monochrome base.
- Success and warning colors appear only in data contexts (response feedback, progress metrics). They never compete with vermillion for attention.
- Dark mode is the default. The app is often used with headphones in focused environments. Dark mode respects that context.

### Typography

**Display / Headings: Noto Sans JP**

Noto Sans JP is chosen for a specific reason: it handles Latin characters and Japanese characters in the same typeface with consistent weight and proportion. In a bilingual app, mismatched fonts between English and Japanese text is jarring. Noto Sans JP eliminates that problem.

| Use | Weight | Size Range |
|-----|--------|-----------|
| App title / hero | Bold (700) | 32-48px |
| Section headings | Medium (500) | 20-24px |
| Body text | Regular (400) | 16-18px |
| Captions / metadata | Regular (400) | 12-14px |
| Furigana | Regular (400) | 8-10px |

**Monospace (metrics/data): IBM Plex Mono**

Used exclusively for performance data: latency numbers, session stats, progress metrics. The monospace signals "this is measured data, not decoration."

**Type rules:**
- Japanese text and English text always use the same typeface (Noto Sans JP) to maintain visual unity.
- Furigana sits directly above kanji with no extra spacing. It should feel integrated, not bolted on.
- Numbers in performance contexts always use IBM Plex Mono, even when surrounded by Noto Sans body text.

### Logo Concept

The Zetto logo is the katakana character ゼ (ze) with the ット (tto) integrated or implied through negative space or geometric reduction. The mark should work at 16x16 (app icon) and 200px+ (marketing).

**Principles:**
- Constructed from clean geometric strokes, not calligraphic brush strokes. Zetto is precise, not artisanal.
- The mark should be recognizable even without the wordmark.
- Monochrome by default. Vermillion accent only in specific branded contexts.
- Must be legible on both dark and light backgrounds without modification.

**Wordmark:** "ZETTO" in Noto Sans JP Bold, all caps, tracked wide. The katakana ゼット can appear below or beside at smaller size as a secondary identifier.

### Iconography

Minimal, geometric, stroke-based. 1.5px stroke weight at default size. Rounded caps. No fills unless the icon represents a "complete" or "active" state (then filled vermillion).

Icons should reference Japanese visual language where natural: a torii-style gate for milestones, a daruma for persistence, simplified kanji radicals as category markers. But never at the expense of clarity. If a Western icon convention is clearer, use it.

### Motion

**Philosophy:** Movement should feel like a metronome, not a bouncy ball. Precise, rhythmic, purposeful.

- Transitions: 200ms ease-out for UI state changes. No bounce, no overshoot.
- Progress animations: Linear timing. Progress bars fill at a steady rate, reflecting actual measured improvement.
- Correct response feedback: A single, clean pulse of vermillion. 150ms. No confetti, no fireworks, no celebration animation.
- Stage advancement (Crawl → Walk → Run → Fly): The only moment the UI allows itself a longer animation. A full-screen transition lasting 1-2 seconds that feels earned, not decorative.

### Imagery and Illustration

Zetto does not use illustrations, mascots, or decorative imagery in the app UI. The interface is the product. Data and text are the visuals.

For marketing materials (landing page, App Store screenshots, social media), photography is preferred over illustration. Specifically:

- Real people in real Japanese environments: train platforms, konbini, izakaya, streets. Never staged stock photography.
- Desaturated with a slight warm tone to match the Washi White / Sumi Black palette.
- Text overlays use the same typographic system as the app. Marketing should feel like an extension of the product, not a different brand.

## Voice and Tone

### Brand Voice

**Confident, direct, specific.** Zetto's written voice matches its tutor personality: warm but not soft, honest without being harsh.

**Characteristics:**
- Uses short sentences. Gets to the point.
- Makes specific claims with numbers. "Your retrieval speed improved 340ms this week" not "You're getting better!"
- Uses physical metaphors over abstract ones. "Hit your reps" not "achieve your goals."
- Dry humor through precision. Never jokes, never puns, never emoji-heavy copy.
- Speaks to adults. No exclamation marks in UI copy. No infantilizing language.

### Tone by Context

| Context | Tone | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| App UI | Functional, minimal | "Session complete. 23 tokens practiced. 4 advanced." |
| Progress reports | Honest, data-forward | "Your て-form accuracy dropped 12% this week. Latency on counters is improving." |
| Marketing | Confident, slightly provocative | "Your Duolingo streak won't help you in Shinjuku." |
| Onboarding | Warm, encouraging, no BS | "Let's figure out where you're at. No wrong answers here." |
| Error states | Calm, constructive | "Connection lost. Your session progress is saved." |
| Push notifications | Brief, useful | "You haven't trained in 3 days. Your 食べる retrieval is probably slipping." |

### Words Zetto Uses

- Train, practice, session, reps
- Produce, respond, speak, say
- Measure, track, progress, improve
- Stage, level, milestone
- Weight, load, difficulty

### Words Zetto Never Uses

- Learn (prefer "train" or "practice")
- Fun, enjoy, play
- Easy, simple, quick
- Streak, points, XP, score, badge, reward
- Master, perfect, fluent (too absolute; prefer "improve," "strengthen," "advance")
- Journey (overused; prefer "progression" or just describe the stages)
- Unlock (gamification language)

## App Store Presence

### App Name
Zetto: Japanese Voice Training

### Subtitle
Train your Japanese. Out loud.

### App Store Description (short)
Zetto measures your spoken Japanese in real time and adjusts difficulty based on how you actually perform. No flashcards. No multiple choice. Just you, talking, getting better.

### App Store Description (long)
Zetto is a voice-first Japanese training app built on one principle: you learn to speak by speaking.

Open the app. Zetto prompts you in Japanese at the edge of your current ability. You respond out loud. The system measures your response time to the millisecond and uses it to decide what comes next. Fast, confident response? The next prompt is harder. Slow, uncertain? It stays at the same level until you're ready.

Your progression follows four stages: Crawl (foundations), Walk (survival), Run (conversational), Fly (fluent). At every stage, your vocabulary cycles through structured exercises, contextual prompts, and open conversation. A word isn't "trained" until you can use it spontaneously.

Zetto also teaches all three Japanese writing systems. Hiragana and katakana from day one. Kanji through radical decomposition: learn the building blocks, and complex characters become readable patterns instead of random symbols.

Free to use. Every feature, every tier. Paid plans just raise the session limits.

### Screenshot Captions (5 screens)

1. "Your tutor listens. Literally." (voice session screen)
2. "Every millisecond is data." (latency metrics view)
3. "Crawl. Walk. Run. Fly." (progression overview)
4. "Kanji, decoded." (radical decomposition view)
5. "No streaks. No gimmicks. Just progress." (weekly audit summary)

## Social Media Strategy

### Platform Priority

1. **TikTok / Instagram Reels** (primary reach)
2. **YouTube** (long-form credibility)
3. **X / Twitter** (community, product updates)
4. **Reddit** (r/LearnJapanese, r/japanese, r/languagelearning)

### Content Pillars

**1. "Say it" challenges (TikTok/Reels, 60%)**
Short clips of real pronunciation challenges. "Can you say this word in under 2 seconds?" Native speaker comparison. No filters, no effects, just a clean frame with the word on screen and a timer. Viewers try it themselves. The implicit pitch: Zetto does this for you, thousands of times, adaptively.

**2. Radical breakdowns (TikTok/Reels/YouTube Shorts, 20%)**
15-30 second clips decomposing a kanji into its radicals. "You see 海? That's 氵(water) + 毎 (every). Water everywhere. Sea." Fast, visual, satisfying. These are inherently shareable because people love feeling like they suddenly understand something that looked impossible.

**3. "Your app lied to you" (YouTube, 10%)**
Longer-form content (3-8 minutes) comparing what language apps teach vs what you actually need. Not mean-spirited, but honest. "Here's what Duolingo teaches you to say. Here's what people actually say in a konbini. Here's the gap." Positions Zetto as the solution without being a pure ad.

**4. Product updates and community (X/Reddit, 10%)**
Ship notes, feature explanations, community discussion. Transparent about how the system works. Open about the SLA research behind design decisions. This builds trust with the serious self-study audience who wants to understand *why* the system works, not just that it does.

### Hashtag Strategy

Primary: #Zetto #SpeakJapanese #JapaneseTraining
Secondary: #LearnJapanese #Japanese #日本語 #JLPT
Avoid: #LanguageLearning (too broad, attracts casual audience)

### Influencer Approach

Partner with Japan-based content creators and Japanese language educators, not generic "polyglot" influencers. The audience trusts people who live in Japan and use Japanese daily over someone who "speaks 12 languages at A2 level."

Priority collaborators: JapanesePod101 educators, Japan vloggers who speak Japanese on camera, ALTs and English teachers in Japan who study Japanese themselves. People whose audience already has skin in the game.
