# The Zetto Manifesto

## Language learning is training, not entertainment.

Every popular language app is built on the same lie: that you can learn a language by tapping bubbles on a toilet. They've gamified the feeling of progress while removing the thing that actually produces it. You get streaks, badges, and leaderboards. You don't get fluency.

Zetto exists because we think that's a waste of people's time.

## The gym, not the game

Think about how progressive overload works at the gym. You pick a weight. You try to hit your rep range. If you're under, you stay at that weight until you get there. Once you hit the range, you move up. You don't skip to a heavier weight because it looks impressive. You don't stay light because it feels comfortable. The system is honest about where you are and relentless about pushing you forward.

That's Zetto. Except the weight is Japanese, and the reps are your voice.

You open the app. Zetto gives you a prompt at the edge of your current ability. You respond, out loud, in Japanese. If you nail it fast, the next one's harder. If you struggle, it stays at the same level until you don't. There are no shortcuts and no cheat codes. There's just you, talking, getting measurably better.

## Why voice-first matters

Most language apps test your ability to recognize correct answers. Zetto tests your ability to produce them. Under time pressure. Out loud.

The difference is enormous. Recognition is passive. You see four options, one of them looks right, you tap it. Your brain barely has to work. Production is active. There's no multiple choice. There's a blank, and you have to fill it with your own voice, from your own memory, in real time.

This is hard. It's supposed to be hard. The difficulty is the point. Every millisecond you spend retrieving a word from memory is strengthening the neural pathway that makes that word available next time. Zetto measures that retrieval time and uses it to calibrate exactly how hard to push you.

But production alone isn't enough. You also need to *hear* Japanese at the right level. Language acquisition research calls this comprehensible input: exposure to language that's slightly above your current ability, close enough to follow, challenging enough to stretch. Every sentence Zetto speaks is calibrated to be that input. The tutor's own speech is the immersion environment, tuned in real time to your demonstrated level. Early on, you're mostly listening: absorbing sounds, patterns, sentence shapes. As you advance, the balance tips toward production and you're carrying the conversation while Zetto's speech becomes the fine-tuning signal. Both sides of the equation are measured, adaptive, and personal.

## Progressive overload for language

Zetto doesn't have a curriculum. There's no syllabus. No "Week 3: te-form" that every learner marches through in lockstep regardless of whether they need it.

Instead, there are four stages that describe where your production ability actually is. The stages change how the AI coaches you. What you practice within a stage depends entirely on what your data says you need.

**Crawl.** You're building the floor. First words, first sounds, first characters. Zetto corrects you immediately and makes you repeat the right form. No ambiguity. Like learning to hold the bar before you load any plates.

**Walk.** You can survive. Order food, ask directions, get on the right train. Zetto starts letting you find your own mistakes before stepping in. The training wheels are loosening.

**Run.** You can hold a conversation. Express opinions, tell stories, disagree politely. Zetto stops breaking character to correct you and instead weaves corrections into the conversation naturally. You're training with a sparring partner now, not a coach with a clipboard.

**Fly.** You sound like yourself in Japanese. Humor, nuance, debate, cultural fluency. Zetto speaks to you at native speed and expects you to keep up. The weight is heavy and you can handle it.

Two learners at Walk stage might be working on completely different things on the same day. One is drilling counters because their data shows consistent hesitation. The other is consolidating te-form because that's where their latency spikes. The system adapts to the person, not the other way around.

At every stage, vocabulary and grammar cycle through three production tiers: structured fill-in-the-blank, contextual prompting, and open-ended conversation. A word isn't "learned" until you can use it spontaneously in a sentence you've never rehearsed. That's the rep range. Until you hit it, you stay at that weight.

## Kanji is not memorization

Japanese has three writing systems, and kanji is the one that scares people off. Thousands of complex characters, each with multiple readings, and most apps teach them as flashcards. Brute force memorization. It works, technically, the same way doing 500 bicep curls with no weight technically counts as exercise.

Zetto teaches kanji the way Chinese schools teach Chinese: radicals first. Kanji isn't random. It's built from about 200 small components that carry meaning. Learn that 氵means water, and suddenly 泳 (swim), 海 (sea), 池 (pond), and 河 (river) all make visual sense. You're not memorizing four unrelated symbols. You're recognizing the water radical and reading the context.

And we never show you a kanji for a word you can't already say. Writing follows speaking. Always. The kanji isn't a new thing to learn. It's a label for something already in your head.

## Why freemium, and why no paywall

Zetto is free to use. The free tier has the same features as the paid tiers. Same AI tutor. Same CWRF progression. Same kanji radical system. Same acoustic telemetry. Same everything.

The difference is capacity. Free users get limits on session length and daily sessions. Paid users get higher limits. That's it. No features locked behind a paywall. No "upgrade to access Run stage." No premium-only grammar lessons.

There is no hard paywall. Ever. You will never open Zetto and see a screen that says "subscribe to continue." You will never find a feature greyed out with a lock icon. You will never be forced to pay for anything. This is a permanent policy, not a launch promotion.

New features ship to paying users first, free users shortly after. That's the reward for supporting the app. It's early access, not exclusive access.

If the economics get tight, we tighten limits or raise prices. We never wall off functionality. A free user with 5 minutes of daily voice time and the full feature set is more valuable (as a potential convert, as word-of-mouth, as a human being learning a language) than a frustrated user staring at a paywall.

This is a deliberate choice.

The mission is to make Japanese accessible to anyone willing to put in the work. If someone in rural Indonesia or a broke university student in Melbourne wants to learn Japanese, the app should let them train with the exact same tools as someone paying for the top tier. They just get fewer reps per day.

You can go from Crawl to Fly without spending a cent. It'll take longer because you're doing fewer sessions per day. The paid tiers aren't buying access to a better product. They're buying more time with the same one.

We'd rather have a million people actually learning Japanese than a hundred thousand people paying for the privilege of pretending to.

## What Zetto is not

Zetto is not a game. There are no streaks, no hearts, no XP bars, no leaderboards. Your only competition is yesterday's version of you.

Zetto is not a textbook. It doesn't lecture you on grammar theory and then quiz you. It throws you into conversation and teaches you grammar when you need it, in the moment you need it.

Zetto is not a translator. It's not going to do the talking for you. It's going to make you good enough that you don't need it to.

Zetto is a gym for your Japanese. Show up, do the reps, and get stronger. That's it.

## The bet

We're betting that people don't actually want to be coddled. They want to be challenged, measured, and shown honest proof that they're improving. They want to walk into a train station in Shinjuku and ask for directions without freezing. They want to sit in an izakaya and follow the conversation.

Every other app bets that people want to feel good about learning. We're betting they want to actually learn.

That's Zetto.
